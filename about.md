## The author

Lance Rossi is a communication theorist and organizational communication scholar whose work sits at the intersection of media systems theory, professional identity, and journalism's democratic functions. He is the founder and executive director of the Foundation for Resilience and Grief Education and Training (FRGET), where he applies communication theory to organizational design and practitioner development. His academic work draws on institutional theory, hybrid media systems scholarship, and media ecology to examine how professional communicators navigate structural change in media environments.

## The project

This project began in June 2026 with a simple observation: Brian Williams showed up on Netflix with a podcast, and the coverage got the story exactly wrong. The adaptation frame — legacy media learns new formats, experienced journalists find new audiences — made a consequential structural event look like a professional development story.

The argument this project develops is different. Legacy broadcast infrastructure is not adapting to the podsphere; it is contracting below the threshold at which it can sustain the media logics it historically carried. The figures it shaped are following the contraction into whatever formats remain viable. That is a divestiture story, not an adaptation story, and the distinction matters enormously for anyone tracking journalism's democratic functions.

The package includes a full-length journal article developing the argument within hybrid media systems theory, a conference paper for the NCA Media and Communication Division presenting the core empirical case, a public essay for the Rossi Communications Substack audience, and a practitioner brief for news executives, independent journalists, and organizational communication professionals. The literature review documents every source the argument draws on. The podcast outline maps the argument into a fifty-minute audio format.

All writing on this site is the author's working draft. The site is part of a systematic practice of developing research concepts in full, across formats, before formal submission — a practice designed to ensure that the argument is genuinely developed before it goes out, and that practitioners have access to the thinking before the academic review cycle completes.
